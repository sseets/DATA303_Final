A Closer Look

Urbanicity

The charts above look at urbanization index and how it corresponds with partisan lean and changes in partisn lean. The first map showcases purely the urbanization index on its own, with the tiered colors allowing us to easily see the pockets of cities and wide expanses of rural land, many of which match up with more conservative regions of the US.

The left scatter plot shows the relationship between partisan lean and urbanicity. Unsurprisingly, districts with higher urban indexes tend to be more democratic while those in rural districts tend to be more republican. This assumption is noted by the positive trend line, indicating a statistically significant (p < 0.0001) positive relationship between the two variables.

The right scatter plot analyzes the question of whether or not urbanicity impacts which way the congressional districts are changing in polarization. For example, are more urban districts becoming more or less progressive? This chart also allows us to examine which types of districts are changing the most. Despite the salience of these questions, the results do not show much definitively. The relatively rectangular shape of the chart indicates that both urban and rural districts are experiencing similar amounts of change in either direction. While there is a slight trend line, suggesting that perhaps more urban districts are becoming more liberal and less urban districts more conservative, this is not a strong relationship. Rather it seems that broad changes in partisan lean are happening at all levels in both directions.

Still, despite the relatively weak correlation between urbanicity and change in partisan lean, that doesn’t mean that there aren’t trends to be discovered. The three sets of bar charts show how the various urban-rural classes have changed on average among the years measured. This chart further exemplifies that there was a much larger comparative difference in lean in 2022 in comparison to previous years, and that more rural districts are trending more conservative while more urban districts are becoming more liberal. More than that, the scale of the chart suggests that those that are becoming more democratic are experiencing a greater shift than those becoming more republican. This chart also shows some interesting patterns when looking at past years. It seems that, from 2020 to 2021, the greatest shift was in rural areas, while from 2018-2021 4/6 of the urban-rural classes became ever so slightly more conservative.

Elasticity

This map looks at the elasticity of the various congressional districts in 2022. Possibly surprisingly, the districts with the highest scores seem fairly spread out, though those with scores below 1–-meaning they tend to move in opposition to the national mood--are primarily in the southeast.
