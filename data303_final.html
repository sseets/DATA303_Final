US Partisan Lean

Background

The purpose of this investigation is to see how partisan leaning has changed across the US in the past couple years. Are we becoming more polarized? What factors are associated with partisan changes, and in what direction?

The purpose of this project is less to explore the exact partisanship of different districts, but to evaluate why certain ones are changing, and in which direction.

Data

The data used for this project comes primarily from FiveThirtyEight’s GitHub site and includes: partisan lean by congressional district for 2018, 2020, 2021, and 2022 (data from 2019 was not available), elasticity scores, and urbanicity data.

Partisan lean is calculated as “50 percent the state or district’s lean relative to the nation in the most recent presidential election, 25 percent its relative lean in the second-most-recent presidential election and 25 percent a custom state-legislative lean based on the statewide popular vote in the four most recent state House elections.” In short, it is a comprehensive measure of a district’s political leaning that analyzes both comparison to the US as a whole and voting history. The scale ranges from -100 to 100, with -100 being a complete Republican lean and 100 being a complete Democratic lean. In addition to these values, I also calculate the change in partisan lean between years. These are simply calculated by subtracting the lean of the previous year from the current year and, as such, range from a scale of -200 to 200. Positive values indicate that the district became more liberal, whereas negative values indicate it became more conservative.

Elasticity represents how sensitive a district is to national political changes. These are not always swing states, but rather states whose partisan leaning or strength might vary drastically depending on the national political environment. The numeric values of these scores represent how many percentage points a district is expected to move towards a party for every point the national mood moves towards that party. For example, a district with a score of 1.10 would be expected to move towards a given party by 1.10 percentage points for every 1.0 percentage points the national mood trends towards that party.

Finally, the urbanization index provides a value and classification for how urban or rural a district is. These values correspond with the following categories:

  • Dense Urban: urbanization index of 13 or higher
  • Urban-Suburban: urbanization index from 12.5 up to 13
  • Dense Suburban: urbanization index from 11.5 up to 12.5
  • Suburban-Exurban: urbanization index from 10.5 up to 11.5
  • Rural-Exurban: urbanization index from 9.5 up to 10.5
  • Mostly Rural: urbanization index below 9.5.

Analysis

In analyzing this data, I investigated the breakdown of each of these factors across the US, mapping them among congressional districts and charting their relationships to one another.

This first map shows how partisan lean has changed from the years 2018-2022. Using the slider and legend on the right, you can cycle through each of the years and see how the partisan lean values change. Those colored in red hues are those that lean conservative, while those in the blue hues are those that lean democratic. Additionally, you can hover over any district and see the name of the house district, the partisan lean for that year, the change from the previous year (excluding 2018), and its urban-rural class. Additionally, each tooltip contains a line chart that shows how partisan lean has changed over the four years. The colors of the line similarly match the legend and can better help visualize which district remained within the same partisanship and which moved. As we can see from the map, the years of 2018, 2020, and 2021 remained pretty consistent, while many districts swung to the right in 2022.

Change Over Time

The map above explicitly shows these changes between years. The values represent the difference between the previous year and current, while the hues similarly correspond with partisan identity (red representing Republican leaning and blue representing Democratic). Here we are able to see the major change from 2021 to 2022 even more clearly, with only slight changes the years before. Additionally, this chart also contains the option to filter based on urban-rural class to allow a deeper investigation into what districts are changing the most. In using these filters, it appears that the largest changes in partisan leaning are coming from the rural districts, which are mostly becoming more conservative, though not as much as one might expect, as well as the urban districts, which are primarily becoming more liberal.

A Closer Look

This chart maps purely the urbanization index, allowing for easy comparison between the previous measures and this one. The tiered colors allow us to easily see the pockets of cities and better see how many of the predominantly conservative regions of the country seem to be rural.

This scatter plot shows the relationship between partisan lean and urbanicity. Unsurprisingly, districts with higher urban indexes tend to be more democratic while those in rural districts tend to be more republican. This assumption is noted by the positive trend line, indicating a statistically significant (p < 0.0001) positive relationship between the two variables.

This scatter plot analyzes the question of whether or not urbanicity impacts which way the congressional districts are changing in polarization. For example, are more urban districts becoming more or less progressive? This chart also allows us to examine which types of districts are changing the most. Despite the salience of these questions, the results do not show much definitively. The relatively rectangular shape of the chart indicates that both urban and rural districts are experiencing similar amounts of change in either direction. While there is a slight trend line, suggesting that perhaps more urban districts are becoming more liberal and less urban districts more conservative, this is not a strong relationship. Rather it seems that broad changes in partisan lean are happening at all levels in both directions.

Still, despite the relatively weak trend, that doesn’t mean that there aren’t relationships to be discovered. The three sets of bar charts show how the various urban-rural classes have changed on average among the years measured. This chart further exemplifies that there was a much larger comparative difference in lean in 2022 in comparison to previous years, and that more rural districts are trending more conservative while more urban districts are becoming more liberal. More than that, the scale of the chart suggests that those that are becoming more democratic are experiencing a greater shift than those becoming more republican. This chart also shows some interesting patterns when looking at past years. It seems that, from 2020 to 2021, the greatest shift was in rural areas, while from 2018-2021 4/6 of the urban-rural classes became ever so slightly more conservative.
